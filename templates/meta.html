Brief Introduction

Frontal cortex(FC) is often referred to as the command and control center of the brain. Higher levels of thinking, such as decision making and self-control, are here. The loss of nerve cells and synapses in AD patients is very obvious, and the loss of neurons in the frontal lobe is unique to AD. However, the accidental factors of a single study are relatively high, the persuasion is not strong, and it is easy to be interfered by various kinds, so the reference is of little significance. Therefore, we collected almost all the original data of mRNA expression chips in various databases which are related to FC and AD, and conducted meta-analysis among these independent researches to eliminate batch effect and accidental error. The results of differential gene research with more biological significance.

Data Source

The data we collected was from GEO dataset, all of which were related to Alzheimer disease for HI tissue studies. There were 3 groups of studies and 67 samples. After data collection and quality control, a total of 3405 genes were meta-analyzed.

Summary of Results

A total of 745 significantly expressed genes were screened (both P values <0.05 and FDR<0.1).

To see more detailed results of this meta-analysis, select and view separately from the navigation bar.

MetaClust Results

Page Introduction

​​MetaClust aims to perform sample clustering analysis combining multiple transcriptomic studies. By integrating information from multiple studies of similar biological purposes, MetaClust can identify a unified intrinsic gene set among all studies, perform weighted clustering analysis using the common intrinsic gene set, and match the clustering patterns across studies to define disease subtypes/cluster types. The resulting clustering from meta-analysis is more robust and accurate than single study analysis.

​The heatmap on top shows the gene-expression profiles of the three studies with selected features. Each row represents a gene, and each column represents a sample. Note that the three studies share a common set of genes. The color bar on top of the heatmap represents the subtype labels. For instance, the black bar on top of each study represents the same subtype for all studies. Clearly, we could see distinct subtype patterns, and these patterns are consistent across studies.

MetaQC Results

Page Introduction

The MetaQC page provides an objective and quantitative tool to help determine the inclusion/exclusion of studies for meta-analysis. More specifically, MetaQC provides users with six quantitative quality control (QC) measures: IQC, EQC, AQCg, CQCg, AQCp, and CQCp. Details of how each measure is defined and computed can be found in Kang et al. (2012). In addition, visualization plots and summarization tables are generated using principal component analysis (PCA) biplots and standardized mean ranks (SMR) to assist in visualization and decision.

​ Figure includes seven columns, with the first six columns corresponding to the six quantitative quality control measures of all studies (a larger value indicates a better quality), and the seventh column is the rank of summary statistics of all the six quality measures (a lower rank indicates a better quality). In addition to the tabular results, MetaQC also generated a PCA biplot based on the six quality control measures, where the circled number is the study index and arrows indicate different measures. If a study (a circled number) is along the direction of majority of the six QC directions, then the study has higher quality and is consistent with other studies. In general, if a study has larger standard mean rank (SMR) values, it is considered to have lower quality and is inconsistent with other studies.

​​MetaNetwork aims to detect gene modules with different co-expression patterns under different biological conditions. We use the correlation to quantify the co-expression level. High correlation in a gene module, either positive or negative, means the genes inside this module are functionally related, which may indicate they are controlled by the same transcriptional regulatory program, or member of the same pathway.

MetaPath Results:Table

Page Introduction

​​The MetaPath module performs pathway analysis by two advanced meta-analytic pathway analysis tools: Meta-Analysis for Pathway Enrichment (MAPE) and Comparative Pathway Integrator (CPI) (Shen et al., 2010; Fang et al., 2017). Instead of directly using the declared DE genes from MetaDE analysis as input, the MAPE algorithm performs pathway analysis in each study and performs meta-analysis on the pathway level. In addition, CPI also includes advanced pathway clustering diagnostics and pathway clustering with text mining to circumvent abundant pathway redundancy in the databases and improve interpretation.

MetaPath has many advantages over common pathway annotation. MetaPath uses more comprehensive and sophisticated methods to jointly perform DE analysis and pathway analysis, and it provides stronger statistical power and more extensive and intuitive biological insights. For example, MAPE G is more similar to common pathway annotation in a sense since both of them combine gene level p-values first and then perform pathway analysis, while MAPE P is different since it performs single-study DE and pathway analysis first and then combines pathway level p-values. MAPE I takes the advantage of both, and CPI is a more advanced algorithm to further perform meta-analysis on studies with known heterogeneities. In addition, MetaPath performs additional pathway clustering to reduce pathway redundancy and extracts the key words from each cluster via the text mining algorithm to assist with the interpretation.

​The heatmap in result shows the -log10 transformed p-value of enrichment analysis in each study. Studies are on columns, and the selected pathways are on rows. The color bar on left indicates group of pathways. In the heatmap, red indicates “more enriched,” and yellow indicates “not significantly enriched.” The color key is on the top left corner. The pathways are sorted by the pathway clusters, as indicated by the colors on the left side of the heatmap.
